## 1. Academic writing style — full "problems to avoid"

Beyond the cross-cutting principles in SKILL.md, watch for:

- **Excessive specialized terminology.** Big words for their own sake read as style over substance
  and make readers doubt you know the material.
- **Inappropriate terminology.** Never use a term whose meaning you're unsure of — don't guess;
  confirm in a discipline-specific dictionary.
- **Personal nouns** (I, me, you, us) overused → the study reads as subjective or as dodging
  empirical evidence.
- **Directives** ("do this", "do that") → reframe as evidence-based recommendations.
- **Informal/conversational tone, slang, idioms, regional dialect** → ambiguous; be direct.
- **Wordiness** → be concise so the reader can't misread your design or purpose.
- **Vague expressions** (they, we, people, "the company", "that area") → edit out while proofreading.
- **Numbered/bulleted lists** → only when clarity demands; each item must be followed by detailed
  analysis, otherwise write systematic prose.
- **Over-description** → some context is needed, but the body should focus on methodology, analysis
  and interpretation of findings, and implications — not background or tangents.
- **Personal experience** → use only as an illustrative example; otherwise it's story-telling, not
  evidence-based writing.
- **Passive vs active voice** is a deliberate choice: use active to foreground the "doer", passive
  to foreground the thing acted upon — not a default.
- **Exception for quotations:** the precision/grammar rules don't apply inside a quote — reproduce
  it exactly, use [sic] for original errors, or paraphrase if it's too vague.

## 2. Improving academic writing (three areas)

1. **Clear writing** — thinking precedes writing; distill and review sources first; write detailed
   outlines; plan and manage time.
2. **Excellent grammar** — learn it properly, practice, seek feedback; keep a style reference
   (e.g. Strunk & White), a college dictionary, and a thesaurus.
3. **Consistent style** — pick one manual (MLA/APA/Chicago) and apply it to numbers, references,
   citations, footnotes, and lists. Some disciplines mandate a style (e.g. education → APA).

## 3. Abstract types

- **Informative** (most common) — a surrogate for the whole paper: purpose, methods, scope + results,
  conclusions, recommendations. Rarely > 300 words.
- **Descriptive** — only describes what the work covers; no results/conclusions; ≤ ~100 words.
- **Critical** — adds a judgment on validity/reliability/completeness; ~400–500 words; rare.
- **Highlight** — a teaser, not balanced or complete; not a true abstract; rarely used academically.

Never cite just an article's abstract — it doesn't demonstrate a thorough review.

## 4. Common grammar mistakes — proofreading lookup list

Hunt for and fix these (English has many exceptions; these are the high-frequency offenders):

- **affect / effect** — affect is usually a verb (to influence); effect is usually a noun (a result),
  occasionally a verb (to bring about).
- **its / it's** — its = possessive; it's = "it is". Not interchangeable.
- **fewer / less** — fewer for countable things; less for uncountable.
- **that / which** — "that" introduces restrictive (essential) clauses; "which" introduces
  non-restrictive (non-essential, comma-set) clauses. In academic prose most "which" clauses should
  become "that" — do a "which hunt."
- **who / whom** — who = subject (substitute he/they); whom = object (substitute him/them).
- **comma splices** — don't join two independent clauses with only a comma; use a period, semicolon,
  or a conjunction.
- **sentence fragments** — a dependent clause can't stand alone as a sentence.
- **semicolons** — join related independent clauses or separate list items containing commas; use
  sparingly.
- **apostrophes** — placement depends on singular/plural; in contractions the apostrophe marks the
  missing letters (is not → isn't). Singular possessives take an apostrophe; plain plurals don't.
- **compared to vs compared with** — "to" implies resemblance across different orders; "with" points
  out differences within the same order.
- **dangling participle** — an opening participial phrase must refer to the sentence's subject.
- **commas around interrupters** — set off interrupting phrases/clauses ("however") with commas; no
  commas around restrictive clauses.
- **existential "this" / "it"** — always give a referent ("this theory…"); be specific.
- **coordinating conjunctions** (and/but/yet) must join elements of equal weight and structure.
- **colorless verbs / bland adjectives** — prefer a stronger, accurate verb over passive + "to be";
  use specific adjectives.
- **unspecific determiners** ("very", "super") — imprecise; cut them.
- **singular words that sound plural** — each, every, everybody, nobody, anybody are grammatically
  singular.
- **subject/pronoun agreement** — don't shift number (singular↔plural) or person (1st↔2nd↔3rd)
  mid-sentence.
- **verb-tense agreement** — don't switch tenses without a reason.
- **capitalization** — capitalize a title before a name (President Taylor), lowercase after (the
  president); capitalize full org names (the Department of Justice), lowercase later refs; minimize
  capitals overall.
- **literally** — means exactly true; not for emphasis/metaphor.
- **lonely quotes** — integrate quotes into sentences; they can't stand alone.
- **split infinitive** — now a style matter, not strictly an error.

## 5. Citing sources — general guidelines

- Don't avoid referencing others — a paper with no references signals unfamiliarity and undercuts
  credibility. References demonstrate command of the relevant literature.
- If your idea was already published, acknowledge it ("see also Smith, 2002"); ignoring it looks
  like plagiarism or a weak review.
- Adapting someone's work still requires citing the original ("adapted from Smith, 1996"; or "based
  on" / "summarized from").
- When several authors share an idea, cite them all to show the breadth of your review.
- If someone has stated your exact point well, quote directly with author, year, and page — and use
  it to reinforce your interpretation.
- Citation generators (BibMe, EasyBib, etc.) aren't foolproof — verify format, spelling, and caps.

## 6. Avoiding plagiarism

**Definition.** Plagiarism is misrepresenting the sources of your information and ideas: presenting
another's written words or ideas as your own; (in experimental work) falsifying data or presenting
another's data as your own; (in speech) quoting others without attribution.

**Forms include (not limited to):**
- Copying whole papers or passages from another student or any source.
- Allowing another student to copy or submit your work.
- Buying/obtaining a paper from any source and submitting it (or passages) as your own.
- Pasting text from the internet or any source without quoting and attributing it.
- Fabricating or falsifying a bibliography.

**Always credit:** another's idea/opinion/theory; any facts, statistics, graphs, drawings, or other
non-textual elements you used or adapted; information that isn't common knowledge; direct quotations;
and paraphrases of another's spoken or written words.
