## 1. Research design

The research problem determines the design, not the reverse. Common design families to recognize:
action research, case study, causal, cohort, cross-sectional, descriptive, experimental,
exploratory, historical, longitudinal, observational, philosophical, sequential.

**Design flaws to avoid (full list):** lack of specificity; poorly defined research problem; lack of
theoretical framework; missing significance ("So what?"); merely summarizing prior research instead
of explicitly linking it to your study; failing to state your contribution to the field;
provincialism (scope/sampling/analysis too narrow to transfer); unclear objectives/hypotheses/
questions; poor or opaque method; proximity (convenience) sampling; undescribed techniques/
instruments; missing statistical treatment in quantitative work; vocabulary problems (jargon,
clichés, culture-specific language); unaddressed ethical dilemmas; unacknowledged limitations and
missing-data effects.

## 2. Choosing a topic — roles of related literature

When reading to refine a topic, related literature can serve as a: **source of criticism** (state a
counter-argument and rebut it); **source of new ideas** (refine your position — but cite); **source
of historical context**; **source of interdisciplinary insight** (view the topic across
disciplines). Keep careful notes of every search and source from the very start.

## 3. The research problem

**Definition.** A statement of an area of concern, a condition to improve, a difficulty to
eliminate, or a troubling question needing investigation. It does *not* prescribe how to do
something, isn't vague/broad, and isn't a value question.

**Four conceptualizations:** Casuist (right/wrong, moral dilemmas); Difference (is there a
difference between groups/treatments?); Descriptive ("what is…?"); Relational (relationship between
variables).

**A social-science problem statement contains:** an engaging lead-in; a declaration of originality
(a knowledge void, backed by the literature review); the central focus; and the significance/
benefits.

**Sources of problems:** deductions from theory; interdisciplinary perspectives; interviewing
practitioners; personal experience; relevant literature (fill gaps, adapt prior methods, replicate
in a new context, or follow authors' "implications for further research").

**Survive "So what?"** — clarity/precision (no sweeping generalizations); identify what's studied
without value-laden words; an overarching question + key variables; key concepts/terms; stated
boundaries; some generalizability; conveyed importance; no needless jargon; more than a descriptive
snapshot.

## 4. Introduction — phases and delimitations

**Three phases:** (1) establish the area (importance / general statements / overview of current
research); (2) identify a niche (oppose an assumption / reveal a gap / pose the question / continue
a tradition); (3) place your study in the niche (intent, key characteristics, important results,
structure preview).

**Delimitations** are the conscious inclusion/exclusion choices that bound scope — aims/objectives,
research questions, variables, methods, and the alternative frameworks you rejected. State both what
you cover and what you deliberately don't, with explicit reasons ("not relevant", "too problematic
because…", "not feasible"). These are upfront choices — distinct from limitations discovered after
the study.

## 5. Literature review

**Six types:** Argumentative (selectively support/refute a position — risks bias); Integrative
(critique + synthesize to generate new frameworks; meets primary-research rigor); Historical (trace
evolution over time); Methodological (focus on *how*, not what — methods, sampling, data, ethics);
Systematic (pre-specified standardized methods for a specific, often cause-effect question);
Theoretical (examine accumulated theory, relationships, adequacy, new hypotheses).

**Structure:** overview + objectives → divide works into themes/categories (for / against /
alternative) → explain similarities and differences → conclude which are strongest and most
valuable.

**Evaluate each work on:** provenance (credentials; evidence behind claims); objectivity
(even-handed vs prejudicial; is contrary data considered?); persuasiveness; value (does it
contribute significantly?).

**Organize by:** chronological/by-publication (only if research clearly builds over time); thematic
(by conceptual category — most authentic thematic reviews break chronological order); or
methodological (by methods used). Beware the informal "third layer" of lore often cited as true
though loosely tied to primary studies.

## 6. Methodology

**Two method groups:** empirical-analytical (objective knowledge, yes/no questions, operationalized
variables, deductive theory→hypothesis, focused on *explanation*) vs interpretative (holistic
understanding, acknowledges the researcher's connection, subjective knowledge, careful
interpretation).

**Problems to avoid:** irrelevant detail; unnecessary explanation of basic procedures (focus on how
you *applied* the method, not on how to do it — exception: unconventional approaches need
justification); problem blindness (document obstacles and how you overcame them); failing to cite
the sources that informed your method; confusing the method with a mere list of information sources.
Organize chronologically (prepare → gather → analyze); use topic sub-sections when detail is heavy.

## 7. Results

**Two organizations:** present all results then a short explanation; or present-then-discuss each
section in turn (common in longer papers). The discussion should follow the same organization.

**Non-textual elements:** place figures/tables either in-text **or** at the back — never both; refer
to each by number in order (Table 1, Chart 1…); keep them distinct from appendix/raw data; number
consecutively; caption goes **below** a figure/table/chart, a title/heading **above**; each element
must stand alone independent of the text. Keep raw data in an appendix unless the professor asks for
it. Report only findings important to the research problem; don't ignore negative results.

## 8. Discussion

**Content elements:** explanation of results (expected or not — go deeper on the surprising);
references to prior research (compare/support); deduction (general application — lessons,
recommendations, best practices); hypothesis (a broader claim for future testing).

**Objectives:** reiterate the problem and state major findings (direct, declarative); explain their
meaning and importance (lead with the most important finding); relate to similar studies; consider
**all** alternative explanations (research discovers, it doesn't prove — don't cherry-pick to fit a
bias); acknowledge limitations before the reader does. Use present tense for established facts, past
tense for specific works. End with principal implications + why they matter + further-research
recommendations (not work you could have done within the study).

## 9. Limitations — full catalogue

**Methodological:** sample size (too small → can't find significant relationships or generalize);
lack of available/reliable data (explain why; frame as future research); lack of prior research
(confirm with a librarian; may force an exploratory design); the data-collection measure (e.g. a
missing survey question); self-reported data (rarely verifiable; biases: selective memory,
telescoping, attribution, exaggeration).

**Researcher:** access (denied/limited — explain); longitudinal/time constraints (a student can't
study for years — pick a feasible topic); cultural/other bias (scrutinize problem framing, data
selection, omissions, ordering, word connotations; acknowledge bias in prior research and how you
avoided perpetuating it); language fluency.

For each limitation: describe it concisely; explain why it exists; explain why it couldn't be
overcome with your method (cite similar studies); and assess its impact on findings/conclusions.
Place at the start or end of the discussion — not buried mid-section. If a study is seriously flawed
(e.g. can't get critical data), consider reframing it as a pilot study and explain how future work
overcomes the flaws — but don't use limitations as an excuse for a weak paper.

## 10. Conclusion — strategies and problems to avoid

**Strategies for a compelling conclusion:** warn of the consequences of inaction; recommend a course
of action; cite a relevant quote/expert (from your lit review); restate a key statistic/fact/image;
(if the discipline allows) a brief reflective narrative; return to an introduction anecdote/quote
and reframe it with your findings; deliver a strong "take-home" message.

**Problems to avoid:** failure to be concise (no method/result detail here — emphasis is on
implications and insight); failure to comment on larger significance (move from your specific problem
back to the general field and your contribution). No new information; no idle speculation.
