# Academic Paper Writing

A working set of constraints for organizing and writing academic research papers. Each rule
below is a thing experienced writers actually check for; the point is to *apply* them while
drafting and revising, not merely to list them.

> Source & attribution: these guidelines are distilled in our own words from Sacred Heart
> University Library's research guide *Organizing Academic Research Papers*, which is based on
> materials by Dr. Robert V. Labaree (USC Libraries). They are general best practices — a specific
> assignment's requirements, or a professor's / journal's instructions, always take precedence.

## How to use this skill

1. **Locate where the user is in the pipeline.** A paper moves roughly: choose topic → fix research
   design → working title → outline → draft sections → abstract (written last) → proofread → cite.
   Jump in at their stage; don't force the whole sequence on them.
2. **Apply the cross-cutting principles** (academic style, evidence, citation) to *everything* you
   write or review, regardless of section.
3. **For the section in play, apply that section's constraints** (below) and run its mini-checklist.
4. **Before "done," run the Final Quality Checklist.**
5. For the exhaustive grammar lookup list, the detailed academic-style "problems to avoid," and the
   full citation/plagiarism rules, read `references/style-grammar-citation.md`. For deeper
   per-section guidance and the design-type and literature-review-type taxonomies, read
   `references/section-reference.md`.

## Cross-cutting principles (apply everywhere)

These define academic writing and apply to every section.

- **One research problem, stated up front.** The whole paper is shaped around a single, clearly
  stated research problem. Every section should make its link to that problem explicit.
- **Thesis-driven, not descriptive.** Start from a position to establish, prove, or disprove.
  Merely describing a topic without research questions is not academic writing.
- **Evidence over opinion.** Opinions must rest on the body of knowledge and be supported by
  academic sources. The strength of the argument equals the quality of the evidence. Present an
  objective, logical case; consider relevant counter-arguments.
- **Formal, precise, concise tone.** Usually third person. Avoid contractions (don't, isn't),
  body-text abbreviations (i.e., e.g.), slang, idioms, clichés, and vague references (they, we,
  people, "the organization", "that area"). Be direct; cut wordiness.
- **Present others fairly.** Describe positions you disagree with accurately and without loaded
  language; state your own strengths confidently but neutrally — not dismissively.
- **Jargon with care.** Use a discipline's specialist terms only when you are sure of their
  meaning and only for an academic audience; never use "big words" for their own sake, and never
  guess at a term — check a discipline-specific dictionary.
- **Cite everything that isn't yours or common knowledge.** Summary, paraphrase, or direct quote —
  if the idea, fact, statistic, or non-textual element came from someone else, attribute it.
- **Lists need analysis.** Use bullets/numbered lists only when clarity demands; each listed item
  must then be followed by real explanation, or write systematic prose instead.
- **One style manual, applied consistently** (APA / MLA / Chicago / Turabian — confirm which one).
- **Quoting suspends the precision rules:** quote exactly as in the source; use [sic] for errors in
  the original; paraphrase instead if the quote is too vague.

## The pipeline, section by section

### Research design (decide before writing)
The research problem dictates the design — never the reverse. The design is the blueprint for
collecting, measuring, and analyzing data so the study actually answers the problem. Avoid the
classic design flaws: vagueness, a poorly defined problem, no theoretical framework, no answer to
"So what?", merely summarizing prior work instead of linking it to your study, an overly narrow
("provincial") scope, unclear objectives/hypotheses, a weak or opaque method, convenience
(proximity) sampling, undescribed instruments, missing statistical treatment, sloppy vocabulary,
unaddressed ethical risks, and unacknowledged limitations.

### 1 · Choosing & framing the topic
- The topic is the organizing principle of the whole analysis. Start early; expect to iterate.
- Identify the key concepts from the prompt (proper nouns, noun phrases, action verbs), then read
  related literature to refine the angle. Seek sources that broaden, modify, *and* challenge your
  position. Keep careful notes of every source from the start.
- Free choice? Ask "What do I want to know?" not "What should I write about?"
- A good research problem/question: is compelling (matters beyond mere curiosity), supports multiple
  perspectives (avoid yes/no dichotomies), is researchable with available resources, has clear
  boundaries and key variables, avoids value-laden language, and survives the "So what?" test. It
  states a concern/question — it does not prescribe a solution and is not a value judgment.

### 2 · The title
- Fewest words that accurately describe content and purpose (~10–15 substantive words). Too long =
  filler ("A Study to Investigate the..."); too short = too general. Drop "study of"/"analysis of".
- Use a **working title** early to anchor focus; write the **final title** after the research is
  done. Name key variables; use current field nomenclature; no abbreviations; proper title case;
  usually a phrase (a question is allowed). Subtitles can add context or qualify scope/time/subject.

### 2 · The outline
- Build a detailed outline before the deep drafting: it exposes gaps, the best order, and whether
  evidence is sufficient — and prevents writer's block and drift. Treat it as a guide, not a cage.
- Choose a topic outline (short phrases) or a sentence outline (full sentences) and stay consistent.
  Steps: state the research problem in one sentence → main categories → first category (often a
  definition or background) → supporting subcategories. For a 15–20 page paper, keep it ≤ 4 pages.
- **Reverse-outline check:** recreate an outline from the draft; if it doesn't read cleanly and in
  order, the paper — not the outline — needs revising.

### 2 · Paragraphs
- A paragraph supports **one** controlling idea: topic sentence + body (evidence/analysis) +
  concluding/bridge sentence. The topic sentence can sit at the start, middle, or end, but it must
  be there and must tie back to the thesis.
- Every paragraph must be **unified, clearly tied to the research problem, coherent, and
  well-developed.** More than one controlling idea (often a paragraph running past a page) → split
  it. Coherence and unity — not length — define a good paragraph. Add internal transitions for
  complex or multi-example paragraphs.

### 3 · The abstract (write it last)
- One paragraph, ≤ ~300 words, in a set order: purpose + research problem → study design → major
  findings → brief interpretation/conclusions. Written **last** even though it appears first; past
  tense; concise complete sentences; active voice where possible.
- Must **not** contain: lengthy background, references to other literature, ellipses/incomplete
  sentences, abbreviations/jargon, or any figure/table or reference to one.
- Test: if a similar researcher could read only the abstract, does it tell the whole story?
- (Most papers use an *informative* abstract — a surrogate for the whole paper. See reference file
  for descriptive/critical/highlight types.)

### 4 · The introduction (often finished late)
- Structure it as an **inverted triangle**: general area → specific niche → your purpose, rationale,
  and potential outcomes. It must answer: What did I study? Why does it matter? What was known
  before? How does this advance knowledge? End with a **roadmap** of the paper.
- Reuse title keywords in the opening lines; give a brief, balanced, cited review of foundational
  literature (not a full lit review); state the hypothesis clearly ("The purpose of this study was
  to…" is fine); justify the chosen design.
- Engage with **one** hook only (story, quote, provocative question, puzzle, or example) — not
  several. Avoid the "dictionary definition" opening.
- State **delimitations** (the inclusion/exclusion choices that bound the scope, with explicit
  reasons) — distinct from limitations, which are discovered after the study.

### 5 · The literature review
- Not a list of summaries: combine **summary + synthesis**, organized into conceptual categories
  (for / against / alternative). Show each work's contribution, relate works to each other, resolve
  conflicts, prevent duplication, identify gaps, and locate your own study in the field.
- Critically evaluate each work on **provenance, objectivity, persuasiveness, and value**.
- Four stages: problem formulation → search → evaluate → analyze/interpret. Narrow the topic to
  keep the source set manageable; clarify expectations (how many sources, what types, whether to
  evaluate) with the professor. (See reference file for the six review types.)

### 6 · The methodology (past tense)
- Answer two questions: how was the data collected/generated, and how was it analyzed. This is the
  basis on which validity is judged. An unreliable method → unreliable results.
- State the overall approach (qualitative / quantitative / mixed) and **show it actually fits the
  problem** — a top deficiency is a method unsuited to the stated objective. Describe data-collection
  methods and the analysis plan; justify subject selection and sampling; give rationale for
  unfamiliar methods; address limitations and confounds openly. Give enough detail to replicate.
- Focus on how you **applied** the method, not on how to *do* the method (exception: unconventional
  approaches need justification). Don't hide problems — document how you handled them. Organize
  chronologically; cite the sources that informed your method choices.

### 7 · The results (past tense, no interpretation)
- Report findings only — no bias, no interpretation — in a logical sequence (usually following the
  methodology). Results don't "prove"; they confirm or reject the research problem.
- Use figures/tables where they present data better; place non-textual elements **either** in-text
  **or** at the back, never both; number consecutively; caption **below** figures/tables, title
  **above**; each element must stand alone. Keep raw data in an appendix unless asked otherwise.
- Don't ignore negative results (document them; explain in the discussion). Be factual and concise;
  avoid vague phrasing ("appeared to be greater than…"); don't repeat the same data.

### 8 · The discussion (interpret; present tense for facts)
- Interpret the findings' significance in light of what was known, and move the reader's
  understanding forward — don't just repeat the introduction. Often the most important section.
- Organize as an inverted pyramid (findings → literature → theory → practice). Restate the problem,
  answer every research question, then for each major finding give the answer, the relevant
  results, then others' work — in the same order as the results section. Interpret unexpected
  findings. Consider **all** plausible explanations, not only those that fit your bias (research is
  to *discover*, not to *prove*). End with the principal implications, why they matter, and
  recommendations for further research (but not work you could have done within the study).

### 8 · Limitations
- Acknowledge them yourself — better than having a grader find them. Don't just list weaknesses;
  **appraise their impact** ("do these problems ultimately matter, and how much?"). Past tense.
- Restrict to limitations tied to your problem; don't apologize for things you never set out to do.
  Common ones: small sample, scarce/unreliable data, little prior research (confirm with a
  librarian), the data-collection measure, self-reported-data biases, access, time constraints,
  bias, language fluency. Place at the start or end of the discussion — not buried mid-section.

### 9 · The conclusion (synthesis, not summary)
- Help the reader see why the work matters after reading. **Synthesize** key points; don't merely
  restate results or the problem. Usually one well-developed paragraph.
- Mirror the introduction in reverse: move from your specific problem back to the broader field and
  your contribution. Restate the main argument and its strongest evidence, point to future research,
  and leave a clear take-home message. No new information; no idle speculation; stay concise.

### 10 · Proofreading
- Revise the big picture (focus, development, arrangement) **before** sentence/word fixes. Set the
  draft aside first; cut inflated wording; know your own recurring mistakes.
- Techniques: work from a printout; read aloud; cover lines below with a ruler; check one error type
  at a time; use find/search for habitual errors; finish with spell-check **and** a slow backward
  read (spell-check misses their/there/they're and "he"/"the"); leave time; have someone else read.

### 11 · Citing & avoiding plagiarism
- Cite to let readers locate sources, to show a thorough review, and to protect your credibility.
  A paper with no references signals unfamiliarity with the field.
- **Plagiarism** = presenting another's words, ideas, or data as your own — including copying
  passages, pasting from the web without quoting/attributing, submitting a bought/borrowed paper,
  and fabricating or falsifying a bibliography. Credit any idea, theory, statistic, figure, quote,
  or paraphrase that isn't yours or common knowledge. When in doubt, cite.
- Adapting someone's work still requires citing the original ("adapted from Smith, 1996"). When
  several authors share an idea, cite them all to show the breadth of your review.

## Final quality checklist (read your draft as the reader)

- [ ] Shaped around one clearly stated research problem, given from the outset.
- [ ] Tells the reader why the problem matters ("So what?" answered).
- [ ] Accurately reports what is (and isn't) already published on it.
- [ ] Provides convincing, well-documented evidence for every claim.
- [ ] Explains how and why the evidence was collected and which theories/concepts were used.
- [ ] Every paragraph carries exactly one controlling idea.
- [ ] Each section's link to the research problem is explicit.
- [ ] Relevant counter-arguments and alternative explanations are considered.
- [ ] Conclusion synthesizes arguments, evidence, and significance (no new information).
- [ ] Formal, precise, consistent style; one citation manual applied throughout.
- [ ] Everything not original or common knowledge is cited; bibliography is accurate.
- [ ] Proofread for flow and for your habitual grammar errors (see reference file).
